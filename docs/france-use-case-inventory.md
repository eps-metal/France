**Primary public source:** https://blob.symtrax.com/doc/fr/tableau-cas-usage.pdf
**Official specs hub:** https://www.impots.gouv.fr/specifications-externes-b2b

## Notes

- Covers use cases 1–42, including sub-cases 17a/17b, 19a/19b, 22a/22b, and separate rows 20 and 21
- One additional working row for **Interco** (intercompany) — not an official DGFiP numbered use case; included as implementation aid
- Counts vary across reference documents because some count sub-cases differently
- Recommended use: scoping, gap analysis, RFPs, PA/ERP workshops, data-model design

## Use Case Table

| # | Name | Business Scenario | Primary Flow Type | E-Invoicing Impact | Reporting Impact | Solution Considerations |
|---|------|-------------------|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| 1 | Multi-order / Multi-delivery | One invoice covers several POs and/or several deliveries | E-invoicing / invoice content | Support many-to-many links between invoice, orders, and deliveries; preserve document traceability | Usually invoice data only; no special payment reporting by default | ERP and billing tools should support multiple references (PO, delivery note, shipment IDs) on the same invoice |
| 2 | Invoice already paid by the buyer | Invoice is issued after the buyer has already paid at time of issuance | E-invoicing + payment status | Invoice must be issued in compliant structured format with a workflow representing an already-paid state | May require payment status transmission or lifecycle status showing invoice is settled at issuance | Need support for paid-at-issue logic, reconciliation, and correct lifecycle/payment status mapping |
| 3 | Invoice to be paid by a third-party payer | A third-party payer is known when the invoice is issued | E-invoicing | Structured invoice must identify the paying third party in addition to the buyer | Reporting focuses on invoice/payment facts; payment actor differs from buyer | Master data model should separate buyer, invoice recipient, and payer |
| 4 | Invoice partially covered by a third party | Part of the amount is paid by the buyer and part by a third party (e.g., subsidy or insurance) | E-invoicing | Invoice must support split-payment responsibility on the same commercial transaction | Payment and reconciliation may need to reflect multiple payers against one invoice | Solution should manage split settlement, allocation by payer, and audit trail |
| 5 | Employee-paid expenses with invoice | An employee advances an expense; invoice is issued in the company's name | Special invoicing scenario | Invoice remains relevant for company accounting and VAT recovery | Potential reimbursement/payment traceability needed internally | Expense management and AP flows should be aligned so invoice ownership and reimbursement are not confused |
| 6 | Employee-paid expenses without invoice | An employee advances an expense with only a simple receipt (e.g., cash-register ticket) | Special expense scenario | May fall outside normal invoice exchange patterns but still requires internal control and tax handling | Often more relevant to evidence retention than invoice exchange | Expense tools should distinguish invoice-based vs receipt-based reimbursement |
| 7 | Purchase paid with lodge card / purchasing card | Purchase paid using a lodge card or purchasing card arrangement | E-invoicing + payment intermediary context | Invoice must align with card-based purchasing model where commercial buyer and payment instrument differ from normal AP | Payment information and reconciliation with card statements are critical | Need mapping between invoice data, card program data, and settlement files |
| 8 | Invoice payable to a designated third party | Invoice is payable to a third party identified at invoicing time (e.g., factor or treasury center) | E-invoicing | Invoice must identify the third-party beneficiary of payment | Reporting of transaction/payment may differ from contractual supplier vs payment beneficiary | Need party-role modeling: supplier, buyer, payee, and potentially service provider |
| 9 | Third-party payer also managing order/reception/invoicing | A known third party manages ordering, receiving, and sometimes invoicing (e.g., distributor or depositary) | Complex intermediary model | Invoice routing and legal/commercial roles must be clearly separated | Transaction reporting must remain tied to the underlying taxable operation | Support complex supply-chain role mapping and approval routing |
| 10 | Beneficiary unknown at invoice creation | Payment beneficiary is unknown when invoice is created (typically subrogation/factoring) | E-invoicing + later lifecycle updates | Initial invoice may not contain final payee details; later changes must be traceable | Lifecycle or payment updates may become important once beneficiary is known | Need ability to update beneficiary/payee information without breaking audit trail |
| 11 | Invoice sent to a third party on behalf of the buyer | Invoice must be received and processed by a third party acting for the buyer | E-invoicing / routing | Invoice routing recipient differs from the legal buyer | Core transaction remains between supplier and buyer; routing adds an extra operational actor | Directory/routing logic should support billing recipient vs legal buyer |
| 12 | Transparent intermediary on the buyer side | A transparent intermediary manages the invoice for the buyer | E-invoicing / routing | Intermediary processes the invoice without replacing the underlying buyer | Reporting should still point to the underlying taxable parties | Need support for transparent agent/intermediary roles and secure transmission path |
| 13 | Subcontracting with direct payment | A subcontractor invoice is paid directly by a third party under a direct-payment or delegation-of-payment model | Specific French construction/public works scenario | Structured invoice must reflect subcontracting and direct payment roles correctly | Payment reporting/reconciliation can differ from standard supplier-buyer settlement | Strong need for role clarity, contract references, and payment delegation support |
| 14 | B2B co-contracting / joint contracting | Joint contractors participate in the same B2B arrangement | Specific invoicing scenario | Invoice content must support co-contracting structures and associated parties | Transaction reporting must remain consistent with the taxable reality | Solution should handle several contractors and contractual role allocation |
| 15 | Order/payment by a third party on behalf of the buyer | A third party places the order and may also pay on behalf of the buyer | E-invoicing with third-party ordering | Invoice must preserve the actual buyer while accommodating third-party ordering/payment involvement | Payment reporting may involve third-party settlement | Need separation of ordering party, buyer, invoice recipient, and payer |
| 16 | Disbursements | A party pays a supplier invoice and seeks reimbursement from another party | Special tax/invoicing scenario | Disbursement handling may fall outside standard resale logic and must be modeled carefully | Reporting treatment can differ from ordinary taxable turnover | Need explicit flagging of disbursement flows and supporting evidence |
| 17a | Payment intermediary | Invoice is payable to a third-party payment intermediary (e.g., marketplace) | Marketplace / intermediary model | Invoice needs to identify the payment intermediary while preserving supplier and buyer information | Payment collection may occur through the intermediary rather than directly to the supplier | Need support for platform business models and payment service provider roles |
| 17b | Payment intermediary + billing mandate | Third-party intermediary both receives payment and issues the invoice under a billing mandate | Marketplace + mandated invoicing | Mandated invoicing and payment intermediation are combined | Reporting and audit trail must show who issued the invoice and for whose account | Need mandate management, issuer identity logic, and strong controls over legal accountability |
| 18 | Debit note | Management of debit notes | Post-invoice adjustment | Solution must distinguish debit notes from ordinary invoices and credit notes | May affect reporting of invoice adjustments and tax amounts | Support adjustment document types and references to original documents |
| 19a | Invoice issued under a billing mandate | A third party issues the invoice on behalf of the supplier under a billing mandate | Mandated invoicing | Structured invoice must represent the legal supplier and the delegated issuer correctly | Reporting must not obscure the accountable taxable supplier | Need mandate repository, issuer attribution, and compliant issuer metadata |
| 19b | Self-billing | The customer issues the invoice on behalf of the supplier | Self-billing | Must support customer-generated invoice flows while retaining supplier legal identity | Reporting must remain aligned with supplier tax responsibility | Need explicit self-billing flag, approvals, and contract-based controls |
| 20 | Advance payment invoice | An advance/deposit invoice is issued before the final invoice | E-invoicing + prepayment | Invoice must support prepayment/down-payment logic | May trigger payment data handling depending on VAT collection rules | Need links between advance invoice and final invoice plus VAT treatment control |
| 21 | Final invoice after advance payment | Final invoice settles or offsets one or more prior advance payments | E-invoicing + settlement | Final structured invoice must reference prior advance invoice(s) and balance due | Reporting should avoid duplicate turnover/tax representation | Need offset mechanics and references to prior advances |
| 22a | Early payment discount – cash accounting VAT | Early payment discount applied to services where VAT is due on collection | E-invoicing + payment timing | Invoice logic must handle discount effects in a cash-accounting VAT context | Payment and collection dates become important for VAT reporting | Need configurable discount logic tied to VAT exigibility at collection |
| 22b | Early payment discount – goods / accrual VAT | Early payment discount applied to goods, or services under accrual VAT/debits option | E-invoicing | Invoice must support discount terms where VAT timing differs from cash-accounting cases | Payment reporting less central than in cash-accounting scenarios, but reconciliation remains important | Need tax-engine rules that distinguish cash vs accrual VAT treatment |
| 23 | Self-billing between private individual and professional | Self-billing flow involving a private individual and a professional | Special self-billing scenario | Non-standard party setup requires careful treatment of issuer and taxable status | Reporting depends on the exact tax treatment of the operation | Need strong party-status validation and eligibility checks |
| 24 | Earnest money / deposit | Handling of earnest money (arrhes) | Special commercial/pre-contract scenario | System must distinguish earnest money from a standard down payment when legally relevant | Reporting and VAT treatment may differ from ordinary invoicing depending on the event | Need event-driven handling and legal/tax classification of deposits |
| 25 | Vouchers and gift cards | Handling of vouchers and gift cards | Special tax scenario | Invoicing logic depends on the voucher/card model and when the taxable event occurs | Reporting may depend on whether the instrument is single-purpose or multi-purpose, or on redemption logic | Need specialized product/tax classification and redemption tracking |
| 26 | Contractual reservation clause | Invoices containing a specific contractual reservation clause | Contract-specific invoice content | Invoice format must allow required contractual text/flags without losing structured compliance | Usually no dedicated reporting beyond the underlying transaction | Template engine should support contractual clauses in both human-readable and structured output |
| 27 | Toll tickets | Toll tickets sold to a taxable person | Sector-specific use case | May require treatment of simplified documents and tax-recovery evidence | Reporting depends on how toll evidence is treated in the invoicing model | Travel/fleet solutions should be aligned with AP/VAT recovery processes |
| 28 | Restaurant receipts | Restaurant receipts issued by a taxable seller established in France | Sector-specific use case | Bridges retail/receipt evidence with invoice or expense needs for business customers | Often relevant for expense and VAT evidence management rather than classic B2B AP automation | Need receipt ingestion, enrichment, and expense-policy alignment |
| 29 | VAT group / single taxable person | Cases involving a VAT group under Article 256 C of the French Tax Code | VAT-group scenario | Supplier/buyer modeling must account for the single taxable person and its members | Reporting logic may differ depending on whether flows are intra-group or with third parties | Need group-level tax identity management and member-level operational routing |
| 30 | VAT already collected / invoice issued afterwards | A B2C e-reporting transaction is invoiced afterwards, after VAT was already collected | E-reporting + later invoicing | Later invoice issuance must not recreate tax already reported/collected | Important to prevent double reporting when invoice is generated after earlier retail/B2C transaction | Need linking between initial e-reporting event and later invoice creation |
| 31 | Mixed invoice | One invoice contains a main transaction and an ancillary transaction | Mixed tax/commercial scenario | Structured invoice must represent multiple legal/tax treatments within a single document | Reporting may require different treatment by line or component | Need line-level tax logic and support for principal/accessory classification |
| 32 | Monthly payments | Handling of monthly payment arrangements | Recurring payment scenario | Invoice timing and payment timing may be decoupled in recurring models | Payment reporting can become important when collection dates drive VAT | Need recurring billing controls and month-by-month reconciliation |
| 33 | Margin VAT scheme | Transactions subject to the margin scheme | Special VAT regime | Invoice content and tax display differ from standard VAT invoices | Reporting must reflect the specific margin-scheme treatment | Tax engine must support margin-regime products and compliant presentation |
| 34 | Partial collection / cancellation of collection | Tracking partial payments and reversal/cancellation of payments | Payment data scenario | Invoice lifecycle must accommodate several collection events and reversals | Highly relevant for payment data reporting where VAT is due on collection | Need event-based payment status model, reversals, and partial allocation logic |
| 35 | Author's statements | Handling of author fee statements | Sector-specific scenario | Non-standard commercial documents may need adaptation to fit invoice/reporting requirements | Reporting depends on underlying tax qualification | Need special document templates and payee/tax treatment controls |
| 36 | Professional secrecy / sensitive data | Transactions subject to professional secrecy and sensitive-data exchange constraints | Confidentiality-sensitive scenario | Invoice exchange must remain compliant while limiting exposed data where required | Reporting obligations still exist, but data minimization and secure handling are critical | Need role-based access, masking, secure routing, and privacy-by-design controls |
| 37 | Unincorporated joint venture | Specific case of French sociétés en participation | Partnership/joint-operation scenario | Invoice party structure can be more complex than a simple one-supplier/one-buyer model | Reporting must remain attached to the relevant taxable person(s) | Need flexible party modeling and robust accounting allocation |
| 38 | Sub-lines and line grouping | Invoices containing sub-lines and grouped lines | Invoice data structure | Structured invoice must retain commercial detail while staying compliant with allowed data formats | Usually invoice-content driven rather than separate reporting logic | Need hierarchical line support and readable rendering for buyers |
| 39 | Multi-seller | A transparent intermediary groups sales from several sellers on the same invoice for one buyer | Platform / multi-seller scenario | Invoice must support multiple underlying sellers while preserving tax/legal clarity | Reporting may need seller-by-seller traceability within one composite flow | Need multi-seller decomposition, settlement allocation, and seller-level auditability |
| 40 | Netting / offsetting | Compensation between cross purchase and sales flows | Settlement scenario | Invoice exchange remains needed even when cash settlement is offset through netting | Payment reporting and reconciliation must reflect that settlement is by offset, not ordinary payment | Need support for non-cash settlement methods and bilateral/multilateral netting references |
| 41 | Barter | Barter transactions between companies | Non-cash consideration scenario | Invoices may still be required even though settlement is not cash-based | Reporting must reflect taxable transactions despite non-cash compensation | Need dual-flow accounting logic and careful tax treatment for non-monetary consideration |
| 42 | Tax refund / duty-free refund | Handling of traveler VAT refund / duty-free refund (détaxe) | Retail/travel special regime | Special documentation and refund workflow may coexist with the invoicing process | Reporting depends on retail/export/traveler refund rules | Need integration with refund operators, traveler evidence, and tax-refund status tracking |
| Interco | Intercompany invoices *(working row — not an official DGFiP use case)* | Invoices between separate legal entities within the same corporate group. If both are French-established taxable persons, treated as domestic B2B and within e-invoicing scope | Domestic B2B / group transaction | If both entities are distinct French-established taxable persons, handle as normal compliant B2B e-invoice despite intragroup relationship | If cross-border or outside domestic French B2B scope, shifts to e-reporting rather than domestic e-invoicing | Solution should distinguish legal entity vs business unit; support intercompany master data, transfer-pricing references, and correct routing by entity scope |
